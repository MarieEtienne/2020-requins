Observation protocol

The observations were made from a plane at 200m altitude and going at an average speed of 180kmh, in the Bay of Biscay off La Rochelle in France. The method used to collect data is a transect sampling, which consists of following lines (transects) and counting the occurences of sharks as well as their distances from the plane.

Sessions

Four sessions were carried out in 2019:

  • Winter : from 12/02 to 27/02
  • Spring : from 30/05 to 02/06
  • Summer : from 31/07 to 08/08
  • Fall : from 25/10 to 19/11

Statistical methods

Detection function

A detection function is used to define the probability of an individual to be observed and take account of observation bias.

Model selection

The selection of the covariates and of the key function was made by minimizing the Akaike Information Criterion.

  • Selected detection covariate: Sea state (Beaufort scale)
  • Key function used: hazard-rate

R function used

Distance::ds

Detection probability formula

\[\normalsize \mathbb{P}(detected \mid present) = g_{\sigma_i}(d) = 1-\frac{1}{e^{(\frac{\sigma_i}{d})^b}}\]

  • b: shape coefficient \[\small \ln(b) = 1.47 \]
  • σi: scale coefficient, impacted by the sea state i = {0, 1, 2, 3} \[\small \ln(\sigma_i) = -2.23 + 0.17 \times i \]
  • d: distance

Density function

Density surface modelling of line transect data is a Generalized Additive Model (GAM)-based approach. It is used to calculate spatially-explicit estimates of animal abundance with data from observations and covariates.

Model selection

To select the density covariates, a forward algorithm minimizing the Akaike Information Criterion was made.

  • Selected density covariates:
    • Sea surface temperature (mean from 4 weeks of measures before the session)
    • Location (X and Y coordinates, in Lambert93)
    • Chlorophyll A concentration (mean from 4 weeks of measures before the session)
  • GAM fitting method: restricted maximum likelihood (REML)
  • Distribution: Negative binomiale, to fit overdispersion

Availability bias

Availability corresponds to the possibility of seing the sharks. It basically represents the proportion of sharks that are close enough to the sea surface to be seen. Three availability bias were taken into account.

  • 1: we consider that all sharks are potentially visible
  • 0.41: the proportion of time blue sharks spend close to the surface
  • on-shelf/off-shelf: a different availability bias whether sharks are on the continental shelf (0.63) or not (0,14)

NB : Two tagged sharks were used to get the availability 0.41 and on-shelf/off-shelf by Nykänen et al., in 2018.

Conclusion

► Shark observation depends on sea state and their presence depends on sea surface temperature, the concentration of chlorophyll A and the location

► No sharks are observed in winter nor fall so there is a possible impact of seasons on the presence of sharks on the surface and/or on migrations.

► In spring, the abundance of sharks is higher but more scattered and in summer the sharks are less abundant but are more concentrated in an area.
Show season comparison

► The availability bias has an impact on the estimated abundance but not on the distribution area.

► The three availability modalities are questionable (1, 0.41 and on-shelf/off-shelf). However, they give relative abundance which can be useful to compare sharks population over time.

Discussion

► The detection function seems odd. Indeed, the more agitated the sea is, the better is the sharks detection; which is surprising. A way to improve it would be to use data from another species with the same detection characteristics (size, behaviour) such as other sharks species or dolphins.

► The estimation precision of the amount of shark is another issue. This could be because only 96 sharks were observed, which is a low-data context. This could be bettered by implementing data from other years or similar areas to fit the model.
